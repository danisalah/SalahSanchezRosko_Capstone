Bring Data Science to
Racial / Ethnic Data Collection

An exploration of the ways that structured racial and ethnic

demographic data collection fails to capture the complex identities

of America's population, and recommendations for how data scientists can

bring more nuanced understandings to collection processes.

Summary

Race is complex, but the nature of collecting and analyzing demographic information requires organizing individuals into categories. If the information we collect is too specific, it becomes analytically challenging to derive any meaningful insights. If the information is too broad, it risks obscuring trends within subgroups of a category.

But collecting this information is important. As one of the largest collectors of American demographic data, The United States Census Bureau uses the results to allocate over $400 billion of funds each year. It becomes critically important then how a group of people is represented under Census criteria in determining their degree of access to resources such as health care, educational facilities, and utility infrastructure. Additionally, the frameworks used by The Census to collect demographic information are widely adopted by external groups in all sectors, seen as the approved standard.

As today's American population becomes increasingly diverse, we wondered if today's US Census categories allowed for sufficient freedom of expression. What could we learn, we wondered, if we allowed people to describe themselves however they liked?

This project seeks to explore how structure racial and ethnic demographic data collection can fail to capture the complex identities of America’s population. We use natural language processing to group users into race/ethnicity categories based on user-inputted free text describing their race, and compare these results to their Census categorization.

Historical Context

=

The terms 'race' and 'ethnicity' are relatively new concepts in American history, and our definitions of them have shifted over time. Today, race is typically associated with a person’s physical characteristics, such as skin or hair color, facial structure, or body type. Ethnicity, on the other hand, refers to a population with shared cultural traits and/or geographical origin. However, these terms are often colloquially used interchangeably, and how an individual self-identifies is additionally complex and unique.

The US Census Bureau today does not discriminate between these two terms, allowing for multiple selections across categories that fall within both definitions. But the categories presented on The Census have been anything but static. Dating back to 1970, the first population census labeled individuals into one of three categories: free white men or women, free non white people, and slaves. These groups didn’t change for 70 years, when a category for American Indians was introduced. Over the next two centuries, groups were added, and in some cases removed, based on the shifting population and needs of the federal government. Today, we have more options than ever before. But do these options sufficiently capture the diversity and complexity of today’s population?

Experiment Design

To explore the question of how well Census categories capture our racial/ethnic identities, we surveyed 4,550 United States respondents, asking them two questions:

1. How would you describe your ethnic/racial identity? Please be as specific or broad as you like.

2. Which category best describes you? Check all that apply.

  • White
  • Hispanic, Latino or Spanish origin
  • Black or African American
  • Asian
  • American Indian or Alaska Native
  • Middle Eastern or North African
  • Native Hawaiian or Other Pacific Islander
  • Other

We analyzed their responses, looking for differences between self-described race and ethnicity, and the categories selected within the Census framework. We ran Natural Language Processing on the free text responses attempting to cluster categories in that way. Our analysis led to several key findings as well as plenty of opportunities for further research.

Results

Given the diverse racial/ethnic makeup of the U.S. population, our hypotehsis going into the experiment was that we would find a large percentage of respondents who would self-desribe in a different way than from the pre-defined Census categories, and would check the "Other" category on the Census question. However, our survey responses did not support this hypothesis. Instead only just over 1% (n=60) of respondents checks the "Other" category, and roughly 62% (n=2,816) of the respondents self-described in accordance with the pre-defined Census categories, for example writing "Asian" then checking the "Asian" category. The other 37% of respondents (n=1,734) describes themselves differently from the pre-defined Census categories, but did not check the "Other" box on the Census question, for instance one might have self-described as Korean but then checked the "Asian" category on the census question.

Our analysis focused on the 37% of respondents who self-described outside of the pre-existing Census categories. Those individuals were typically doing so for one of three reasons:

1. They are looking for more specific categories (typically based on geography or place of origin). This is evident in the below visual, showing the top terms used in the self-description field within the Asian Census category.

2. They identify as American or a variant thereof. Of those who used the term “American” as a descriptor in their self-description, the majority of them selected the White category, but certainly the American identity was present across the majority of ethnic groups.

3. Their identity doesn’t fit into a category (mixed race, religious identification, other). This group of individuals is necessarily the most difficult to analyze, as their descriptions are varied, diverse, and often cross ethnic categories.

Conclusion

At the start of this research, our goal was to provide a recommendation for additional categories to be included in The Census in the future. While in our analysis no single groups were clearly identified, we ultimately have some direction for future research.

The groups for which a more specific Census category would add the most value are those whose identities are markedly different from their broader racial/ethnic categories. While these groups may be relatively small by volume, they stand to benefit the most by targeted recognition in Census results through additional resource allocation or program access.There is a myriad of criteria that could be used for determining these groups requires, and significant further research is required.
